Meet Mr. YOLO

A story about the developer who ships first, asks questions never, and leaves behind solutions that work perfectly — until someone else has to touch them.

Ahmed El-Khatib
March 2026
8 min read

4:47 PM. Thursday. Two days before the client demo.

Mr. YOLO opens the Power Apps maker portal. In the top-right corner, it says Default environment. He notices this the way you notice a speed limit sign on the motorway — registers it, files it under not relevant right now. He thinks: it's just a prototype. I'll move it later.

He doesn't move it later.

Six months pass. The prototype is in production. There are forty-three users. There are three cloud flows. There is a canvas app with nineteen screens, a patch formula inside a timer control that runs every sixty seconds, and a SharePoint list being used as a relational database because it was faster to set up. There are no environment variables. The service account email is hardcoded in eleven flows. Nobody knows the publisher prefix — or what a publisher prefix is. The word "solution" on this project refers to a ZIP file that one developer keeps on their desktop, updated manually, emailed to the client, and imported through a browser window whenever something needs to be "deployed."

And somewhere in a Jira board that nobody opens, there is a ticket that says: TODO: move to proper environment.

It was created six months ago. It has never been touched. It is assigned to Mr. YOLO.

§ 01

Who Is Mr. YOLO?

Mr. YOLO is not a villain. He is not incompetent. He is — in every measurable short-term sense — effective. He delivers. When the business needs a form-to-SharePoint flow by Friday, he has it running by Thursday afternoon. When a manager asks for a dashboard, he builds one before the manager has finished explaining what they want. He doesn't slow things down with process. He doesn't open tickets for every decision. He ships.

The problems start when someone else inherits his work. When the business decides the prototype was actually production-ready. When the project grows from one developer to five. When the client asks for a second environment. When an auditor asks which version is currently deployed. When the senior developer who joins in month three asks: where is source control? What is the publisher prefix? Is there a deployment pipeline?

Mr. YOLO's answer to all of these is technically honest: it works. Don't touch it.

The Mr. YOLO trait inventory

01
Works exclusively in the Default environment. It's always there. It already has Dataverse. Nobody told him not to. The default environment is the path of least resistance, and Mr. YOLO is efficient.
02
Has never opened a solution ZIP file. He exports it as a black box. He imports it as a black box. The file between those two actions is someone else's concern.
03
Deploys by dragging exports between browser windows. Admin Center tab on the left, source environment on the right. Export, download, switch tabs, import. This is his pipeline. It works until it doesn't.
04
Uses one solution for everything. The data model, the flows, the canvas apps, the security roles, the dashboards, the PCF components — all in one solution named ProjectName_v3_FINAL_real.zip.
05
Hardcodes everything that could be an environment variable. The API endpoint. The SharePoint site URL. The service account email. The lookup GUID he copy-pasted from the browser URL bar eight months ago. It's fine.
06
Has no DLP policy. There was never a right time to set one up. The connectors that shouldn't be in this environment are in this environment. Nobody has looked. It's probably fine.
07
Thinks ALM is something large enterprises do. This project has six users. ALM is overkill right now. He'll add governance later, when it matters. It will matter in two months. He will add it never.

If you have worked on any Power Platform project with more than one developer and more than one environment, you have met Mr. YOLO. You may have been him at some point. I know I was — earlier in my career, when the platform was new to me and the path of least resistance looked a lot like the right path.

A note on the character

Mr. YOLO is a composite. He is not any one person, and he is not a criticism of any individual developer. He is the product of time pressure, unclear requirements, missing guidance, and a platform that makes it dangerously easy to start building before you know what you're building toward. Every mistake in this series is a mistake I've seen on real projects — including on projects I was responsible for.

§ 02

Why This Series Exists

The Power Platform is genuinely remarkable at shortening the distance between an idea and a working solution. That is its strength. That is also the source of most ALM problems on the platform.

Because it is fast to start, teams start without deciding how they'll finish. Because it's visual and approachable, projects grow past the complexity threshold where informal processes still hold — and nobody notices until go-live. Because Microsoft's documentation is thorough on individual features but doesn't connect the decisions: what to configure in what order, why the sequence matters, and what breaks permanently when you get it wrong.

This series is the document I wish had existed when I started building on the platform. Not a certification guide. Not a feature walkthrough. A practitioner's sequence — the decisions, in the order you'll actually face them, with the context you need to make them well the first time.

Who this is for

You're a developer who has shipped solutions but never built a proper pipeline. You know the platform well. You don't know why your deployments are painful, or how to make them not be.

You're a technical lead inheriting a project that Mr. YOLO started. You need to understand what decisions were made, what they cost to reverse, and what to fix first.

You're a Power Platform admin establishing governance on a tenant where things have already gotten messy.

You're starting a fresh project and want to do it right from day one. This series gives you a sequence to follow and a checklist to validate before you write a single formula.

A First Look Inside the ZIP

Mr. YOLO has never opened a solution ZIP file. He exports it, downloads it, and imports it somewhere else. The file between those two events is a black box — goes in one end, solution comes out the other. This is extremely common. Most Power Platform developers have never read a single line of what's inside.

The ZIP is not magic. It's a structured archive: a folder of XML files, each one a precise, machine-readable description of every component in your solution. The root of the archive is solution.xml — a manifest that defines the solution's identity, its publisher, its version, and an enumerated list of every component it contains.

Here's what a real one looks like:

solution.xml — extracted from a managed solution export
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="utf-8"?>
<ImportExportXml version="9.0.0.0"
  SolutionPackageVersion="9.0"
  languagecode="1033"
  generatedBy="CrmExport">
  <SolutionManifest>
    <UniqueName>ContosoCore</UniqueName>
    <LocalizedNames>
      <LocalizedName description="Contoso Core" languagecode="1033" />
    </LocalizedNames>
    <Version>1.2.0.47</Version>
    <Managed>1</Managed>
    <Publisher>
      <UniqueName>contoso</UniqueName>
      <CustomizationPrefix>con</CustomizationPrefix>
      <CustomizationOptionValuePrefix>10000</CustomizationOptionValuePrefix>
    </Publisher>
    <RootComponents>
      <RootComponent type="1"  id="{4a5f2b3c-8e1d-4f9a-b7c2-1e3d5a9f8b7c}" behavior="0" />
      <RootComponent type="61" id="{e8d1c9a7-3f2b-4e5d-a6c1-9b8f7e2d1c4a}" behavior="0" />
      <RootComponent type="29" id="{f3c1d8b2-7a4e-4f9c-b5d3-2e6a8c1f9d7b}" behavior="0" />
      <RootComponent type="92" id="{b7d4e9a1-2c6f-4b8d-a3e5-9f1c7b4d2e8a}" behavior="0" />
      <!-- ...43 more components... -->
    </RootComponents>
  </SolutionManifest>
</ImportExportXml>

Each RootComponent is one component in your solution. The type number is a component type code — 1 is an Entity (Table), 29 is a Saved Query, 61 is a Form, 92 is a Plugin Assembly. The id is the component's GUID in Dataverse. Every component type has a corresponding folder in the ZIP with its own XML file.

This is the file that encodes your publisher prefix — permanently, across every component. This is the file where two developers exporting from different environments and trying to merge solutions will find their first conflict. This is the file where the difference between Managed>0< and Managed>1< determines what a receiving environment can and cannot edit.

The bonus post in this series is a complete anatomy of everything inside the archive: how solution.xml works, what each component type maps to in the UI, how the customizations folder is structured, what changes between managed and unmanaged exports, how patches work at the file level, and what actually happens inside the ZIP when a merge conflict occurs. It is the post that will change how you think about exports, patches, and source control for Power Platform solutions.

Why it matters now

You don't need to understand every file in the ZIP before you start building. But you need to know that the ZIP has structure — and that structure is a record of every architectural decision you've made, including the ones made by accident. The bonus post gives you that mental model. Read it before you write your first patch.
